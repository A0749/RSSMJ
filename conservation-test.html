The Museum's Tale

Read the Story ↓
RAJA SHANKAR SHAH
KUNWAR RAGHUNATH SHAH
TRIBAL FREEDOM FIGHTERS MUSEUM
↓

In January 2022, the Department of Tribal Affairs, Govt. of Madhya Pradesh approached INTACH to repair and restore a dilapidated historic building in Jabalpur and reuse it as a museum dedicated to the life of Gondwana King Raja Shankar Shah and his son Kunwar Raghunath Shah, both staunch freedom fighters. King Shankar Shah was the ruler of the Garha Kingdom in Gondwana. He led the freedom struggle against the East India Company in Jabalpur.

He and his son Kunwar Raghunath Shah stood up to British Commissioner, E. Clark who was imposing unfair taxes on the landlords and tenants. They were martyred on 18th September 1857. The day holds an immense emotional significance for the community to date. The museum’s core purpose is to highlight the remarkable lives and contributions of Raja Shankar Shah and Kunwar Raghunath Shah during the 1857 revolt against British oppression. With five thoughtfully curated galleries, the museum endeavors to offer a balanced, critical, and reflective narrative, utilizing state-of-the-art technologies, artefacts, and installations. The museum is a tribute to the heroic sacrifice of our lesser-known freedom fighters.

Jabalpur, an ancient city in Madhya Pradesh along the Narmada River, blends cultural influences from various dynastic rulers and the British colonial period with the natural beauty of the the marble rocks at Bhedaghat. Its history stretches from the Palaeolithic era to the British period. The city was ruled by the Kalachuris (8th to 13th centuries) and the Gond Rajas (13th to 18th centuries) before the Marathas and British. Architectural legacies include manmade water bodies like Ranital and Adhaartal, temples such as Chausath Yogini, and Madan Mahal fort. The Marathas ruled the city until 1817, when the British took over, leaving their mark in the form of expansive cantonment, Civil lines, colonial residences, and barracks. Today, Jabalpur is a key administrative, industrial, and commercial hub in Madhya Pradesh.

The Divisional Forest Officers (DFO) complex in South Civil Lines, near the railway station, was selected for the Museum. Civil Lines is a key colonial area in Jabalpur with 19th-century administrative buildings such as the High Court of Madhya Pradesh, Central Jail, and DRM office. The area features broad avenues and well-planned chowks like the High Court Chauraha and Mal Godaam Chowk, which has statues of Raja Shankar Shah and Raghunath Shah. It also includes colonial-era sites like Model High School, Christ Church Higher Secondary School, Raja Gopaldas Dharamshala, the Clock Tower, and the Satpuda Bridge. Open spaces such as the police ground, railway stadium, and Omti Nala add to the area's appeal. This mix of administrative and recreational spaces underscores the area's importance in Jabalpur's colonial and post-colonial development, making it an ideal location for the museum.

Building Restoration

The former DFO building, an early 19th-century brick masonry structure with a high ceiling and truss roof, was likely an important British administrative office and residence. Its original sandstone flooring added to its historical value. However, over time, various alterations and additions, such as new rooms and toilets, significantly modified its original layout. Walls were added in low-height rooms, closing several original openings, and brick pillars in the verandahs were altered or dismantled. Structural cracks, dampness, and neglect led to wooden decay, metal rusting, plaster flaking, and vegetation growth. By the time INTACH visited, new constructions around the building obscured its heritage character. Only after removing these new additions was the original historic structure revealed. Restoration work began in May 2022, with a target completion date of September 2023, involving detailed drawings and dismantling.

The one-acre site was fully developed around the historic building. Five RCC buildings were later added, which were ruined and uninhabited, along with a shaded structure, were demolished first. This was followed by carefully dismantling reconstructed portions of the historic building and recreating its original masonry, including tall brick pillars. Restoration work was extensive and aimed at preserving the building’s authentic form. It included masonry repairs, stone restoration, flooring refurbishment, service upgrades, waterproofing, and anti-termite treatment over a year. Structural consolidation of the cracks involved the use of steel (SS) bars, chicken wire mesh, and stone blocks.

The RCC roofs added on the verandahs that undermined the original façade of the building were restored to the traditional roofing system. Structural repairs required proper support and propping of the structure for a longer period. Walls covered with enamel, wallpaper, vinyl etc. were restored to lime plaster, and ceramic tiles were replaced with sandstone. Antique tiles were relaid meticulously. Wooden and jack arch ceilings were restored to their original systems, and all wooden doors and windows were repaired, polished, and fitted with glass panes.

The restoration not only repaired damages but also fortified the structure for long-term stability, emphasizing local materials and traditional technologies. The conservation efforts included structural preservation and adaptive reuse of the building.
