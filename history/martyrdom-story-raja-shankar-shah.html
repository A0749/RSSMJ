Martyrdom Story of Raja & Kunwar

RAJA SHANKAR SHAH
KUNWAR RAGHUNATH SHAH
TRIBAL FREEDOM FIGHTERS MUSEUM
↓

‘At the head of the conspiracy was Shunker Shah, the Gond rajah, and his son. Their place of abode is about four miles from Jabalpur. In former days, this family ruled over all this part of the country; they can trace their descent for sixty generations. The family had been deprived of everything by the Mahrattas and were in great poverty when we took possession. Our government raised them up from this state and gave them sufficient to support themselves comfortably; and now they showed their gratitude by conspiring against us in our time of sore trial.

The family have neither much property nor power, but the ancient name and prestige was a point on which to rally. On the 18th, at 11 o’clock A.M., our two guns were advanced a few hundred yards in front of the Residency, covered by a company of the 33d and a few troopers. It became known that the Gond rajah and his son were about to be blown away from the cannon’s mouth.

Despite this internal conflict, Raghunath Shah and his father, Raja Shankar Shah, united in their opposition to British colonial rule. Their struggle against the British was part of a broader resistance movement in central India, where local rulers and leaders were increasingly disillusioned with the East India Company’s oppressive policies. In Jabalpur, Raghunath and his father fought to drive the British out of their land, committing themselves to the cause of Indian independence long before the major uprising of 1857. Their anti-British plan, however, were discovered by the colonial authorities. Both father and son were arrested, and in a harsh display of British might, they were sentenced to death.

The old man walked up to the guns with a firm stride; the son appeared more dejected. The old man, with his snow-white hair and firm manner, almost excited compassion. One had to remember, before such feelings could be checked, how atrociously he intended to deal with us had his conspiracy succeeded; the evidence of his guilt was overwhelming.

All was over in a few minutes. The scattered remains were pounced upon by kites and vultures, but what could be collected was handed over to the ranee.’

(Dodd, G. (1859). The history of the Indian revolt, and of the expeditions to Persia, China, and Japan, 1856-7-8 [signed G.D.]. United Kingdom: éditeur non identifié, pp. 346)
